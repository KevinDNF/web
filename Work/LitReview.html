Mitigating corporate information on the web - The implication of botnets in corporate cyber-security.

Abstract

The aim of this paper is to present a short analysis of the current security flaws in corporations, by analysing modern techniques used by cybercriminals, their adaptation of botnets into cybercrimes and comparing existing solutions employed by corporations.

The importance of corporate security

Due to the increasing amount of data corporations hold and gather, a security concern from the public has risen, as the data organisations hold is no longer just stock quantities and record but instead personal user data. More and more corporations require the user to register for an account to allow them to use their services or benefits, giving personal information and login details. This requires the customer to trust organisation with their personal data. Studies have shown that it is a challenge to regain the trust of customers after their data have been exposed. This is showed in (Whitler & Farris, 2017) where they explain many cyber-attacks and its long-term impact including the more recent Target's attack which is estimated to have cost over $1 billion and 50 years of trust. Therefore, it is imperative for organisations to understand the vulnerabilities in their system and how criminals, exploit these, to make sure that they are taking the correct precautions to avoid data leakage.

Introduction to botnets and their impact on corporate security.

As technology develops so does the techniques cybercriminals use to break into systems, one way cybercriminals have adapted to the modern technologies is by moving from malware attacks to utilizing the processing power of multiple machines around the world for their malicious tasks called botnets (Schultz, 2006) which are a collection of infected machines which have a program running the background with malicious intends without the user knowledge. These programs work in a command and control manner where the botherder is the administrator who sends instructions to the other infected machines known as zombies. The infected machines can then be instructed to perform attacks against other machines or transmit personal data outside of the company. (Elliott, 2010)

The implications of botnets in account authentication attacks

It is common for employees to login into their cloud service or private storage using a username and password, this user credentials must be kept secure and confidential to avoid having unauthorised access to the organisation’s data. This point of entry can be exploited by cybercriminals to gain access to their sensitive data.

Brute force attacks

Criminals with the intent of breaking into someone's account might first attempt to guess the account password, this technique has greatly improved by the introduction of botnets in which a more elaborate approach such as a brute force attacks attack can be used. These attacks, are orchestrated attacks in which the criminal will attempt to login into the users' account by using a script that will test as many passwords as it can in a very short time these passwords can be generated using commonly used characters or with a dictionary containing common passwords and words. Usually, login services provide some protection against this by having a maximum number of login attempts per computer which is why botnets are used to counter this protection. (Elliott, 2010)

Phishing attacks

If the password cannot be easily cracked criminals might opt to use social engineering techniques to get personal data. Phishing is a social engineering technique where the criminal tricks someone into giving his personal information without knowing the criminal intent, which is usually done tricking the user into believing that he is giving the information to a reputable source. This attack can be fake emails trying to impersonate a company or impersonating an individual in which the target user might trust, inside this email the criminal might be asking for the user's personal information or make them click on a fake website where they might use their login details as if it was the real one. Phishing can be less direct via a DNS spoofing attack where the domain lookup is compromised so that it leads to the criminal website. (Issac & Chiong, 2006) Since phishing requires to trick the user, targeted attacks are not usually successful, but criminals will perform instead a mass attack using botnets aimed at a lot of users.

Comparing viable solutions against authentication attacks

Password strength

To minimize the chance of a brute force attacks from successfully guessing user passwords it is recommended by multiple sources (Ji et al., 2017) & (Shen, Yu, Xu, Yang, & Guan, 2016) to use longer passwords with characters from three or more symbol sets. According to these studies, users tend to use passwords which are more convenient to remember and are easier to type on a standard keyboard. This means that criminals can build up a dictionary based on these features and use previous password leakage, like from the Yahoo incident (Whitler & Farris, 2017) allows them to easily crack them. Avoiding these common tropes ensures that the password strength is secure enough to avoid a standard brute force attack.

Phishing countermeasures

The most common technique to protect corporations from phishing attacks is by training each stakeholder who has access to the sensitive data login credentials so that they can identify when an email or website might be fraudulent. This has been previously tackled by performing rule-based training in which the user will have to follow a set of rules in which include, never clicking a link on an email or giving away information without checking the validity of the request, employees should also perform inspections of the website/email for the details that might prove that is fake before carrying on with their task. (Issac, Chiong, & Jacob, 2014) but according to (Jensen, Dinger, Wright, & Thatcher, 2017) research, the overuse of this technique in the workplace has to lead the employees with an overconfidence in analysing and identifying phishing attacks leading them to be more susceptible to fail at identifying a phishing attack. Instead, the paper recommends using a mindful training, which promotes the idea of pausing and focusing on the environment and situation to avoid overconfidence. This combined with rule-based training is their conclusion to the current problem with rule base training.

Conclusion

In this paper, we described different techniques used by cybercriminals, their adaptation to botnets to improve their previous attacks and analysed different techniques employed in the workplace. We concluded that currently the best solutions to protect from brute force attacks is by using long passwords containing symbols from multiple character sets and that overuse of training in the workplace can be counterproductive. This paper shows that it is imperative for organizations to stay up to date with possible security flaws as technology advances so do criminal techniques to bypass previous security.
